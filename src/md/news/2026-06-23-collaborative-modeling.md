We are pleased to announce our latest publication, a Community Page in PLOS Biology on how web-based platforms can bring experimentalists and computational modelers together during the earliest, most exploratory stages of model development.

Interdisciplinary consortia often struggle to collaborate on models: modelers work with code and finished results, while experimentalists are left waiting until a model is "done" before they can engage with it. We argue that browser-based, interactive prototypes can close this gap by letting the whole team explore and shape a model as it evolves, rather than only after the fact.

Based on our own experience, we propose four design principles for such platforms: **transparency** (variables, parameters, and equations should be readable by non-modelers, not hidden in a black box), **immediate responsiveness** (low-latency calculation and a responsive interface for dynamic exploration), **accessibility** (no local installs, dependencies, or coding knowledge required), and **support for asynchronous iteration** (parameter variation and model fitting that consortium members can explore independently between meetings).

We illustrate these principles with two platforms developed in our lab: mxlweb, which runs ODE models for microbial community research directly in the browser, and ModelXplorer, which supports ODE model editing, data import, and fitting for photosynthesis research. Our central takeaway: a platform that experimentalists actually use, even in a limited way, contributes more to a collaboration than a feature-rich system that stays in the hands of modelers alone.
